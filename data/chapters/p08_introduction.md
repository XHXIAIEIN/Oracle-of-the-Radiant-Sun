# Introduction

The study of the world around us started with the first observations made by our early ancestors of the apparent movement across the sky of the Sun, Moon and, later, the planets. The development of the understanding of the cycle of seasons made it possible for mankind to predict the dying off, rebirth and flowering of all living things on earth. The yearly cycle became the predictable rhythm of life. Consequently, the appearance of the great radiant orb of the Sun took on the form of an all-powerful god presence to be worshipped and appeased. The four great periods of the year — the vernal equinox, the summer solstice, the autumn equinox and the winter solstice — became the core times for worship of the Sun god, and the reliability of the god's circuit of the heavens was registered and measured in the erection of great stone monoliths and lines of dolmens marking the path of the deity.

The same powerful, life-giving Sun god has existed in one form or another in religion after religion, each new deity replacing the one before and using exactly the same symbolic periods that mark the Sun's calendar. The winter equinox traditionally marks the birthday, and the spring equinox the death and rebirth of male Sun deities such as Apollo, Mithras, Baal, Dionysius, Osiris and countless others. Most current religions continue this tradition. It is not without significance that many Western countries make their religious observances on the Sun's day, Sunday.

## The Birth of Astrology

The apparent path of the Sun across the sky and through the twelve constellations was defined in ancient times, and predictions were made on the nature of the effects on the earth of these movements. These became translated into what we know as astrology, and most people are aware of, even if they are not entirely comfortable with, the interpretation of the zodiac sign in which the Sun was placed at their birth. The Sun's path is not now measured against the exact positions of the constellations, but in thirty-degree sections around the path of the ecliptic, starting at the spring equinox, or the beginning of the sign Aries.

Worship of the Sun in a more worldly sense has seen a vast growth over the last century in holiday sun-seekers, who, despite the well-reported dangers, cover themselves in oils and lotions and bask in the divine heat and light of our flaming star. The fact that most of these sun-drenched resorts are by the sea confirms and symbolizes our unconscious and continued worship of the fire/water essence of mankind's two most powerful deities, Sun god and Moon goddess.

The radiant Sun illuminates and brings to life all living things on earth; in the same way, the tradition of divination, through the position of the Sun and its small group of planets in the heavens, brings illumination and life to the predictions we make in its name.
